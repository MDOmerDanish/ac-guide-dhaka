## 🧮 2. Tonnage (BTU) Capacity Calculation

To ensure energy efficiency and durable operation, we calculate the required cooling capacity (measured in BTU/hr) using standard heat-load engineering factors:

1.  **Base Cooling Capacity (Area-based):** 
    *   *Calculation:* 250 sq ft × 80 BTU/sq ft = **20,000 BTU/hr**
2.  **Top Floor Multiplier (+15%):** 
    *   Top floors absorb massive radiant heat from the concrete roof slab throughout the day.
    *   *Calculation:* +3,000 BTU/hr
3.  **High Ceiling Multiplier (+10%):** 
    *   Your 10 ft ceiling increases the total air volume to cool by ~10% compared to standard 9 ft ceilings.
    *   *Calculation:* +2,000 BTU/hr
4.  **Windows (2) & Doors (1) Multiplier:**
    *   Each window adds ambient heat leakage.
    *   *Calculation:* +2,000 BTU/hr
5.  **Occupant Load (1 Person):**
    *   *Calculation:* +600 BTU/hr

*   **Total Calculated Heat Load:** **≈ 27,600 BTU/hr**

### 🏷️ Recommended AC Size: **2.5 Ton (30,000 BTU/hr)**

*   **Why 1.5 Ton (18,000 BTU) is too small:** It will fail to cool the room during peak summer afternoons. The compressor will run continuously at 100% capacity, resulting in very high electricity bills and premature compressor wear.
*   **Why 2.0 Ton (24,000 BTU) is risky:** It might suffice on cloudy or cooler days, but will struggle on top-floor rooms in Dhaka during extreme summer heatwaves (38°C–42°C).
*   **Why 2.5 Ton (30,000 BTU) is the Sweet Spot:** It provides a safe 2,400 BTU headroom. With an **Inverter**, this extra power allows the AC to cool the room down to your set temperature in just a few minutes, after which the compressor slows down to a low-power state. This is where you achieve the 50% to 60% electricity savings.
